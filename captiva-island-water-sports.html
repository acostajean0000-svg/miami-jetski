Captiva Island — Old Florida at the Edge of the Gulf

Captiva Island sits at the northern tip of Sanibel Island, connected by a short bridge across Blind Pass. Together, Sanibel and Captiva form one of the most celebrated barrier island destinations on Florida's Gulf Coast — world-famous for shelling, wildlife, and a low-key, Old Florida character that sets them apart from more developed resort towns.

Captiva's water world is anchored by Pine Island Sound to the east — a vast, shallow estuary protected from the Gulf by the barrier islands. This sound is one of the finest inshore fishing and wildlife-watching waters in all of Florida, filled with snook, redfish, sea trout, and tarpon, and regularly visited by manatees and dolphins. To the north and west, accessible only by boat, lie some of Southwest Florida's most spectacular wild destinations: Cayo Costa State Park, North Captiva Island, Useppa Island, and Cabbage Key.

Captiva and neighboring Cape Coral have a solid selection of boat rental and charter operators — making it easy to explore this remarkable island chain on your own terms or with an experienced local guide.

🐚 Shelling Runs to Cayo Costa

Cayo Costa State Park — accessible only by boat from Captiva — has some of the best shelling beaches in the entire state of Florida. The park's Gulf-facing beaches are littered with lightning whelks, horse conchs, sand dollars, and dozens of other species. Boat rentals from Beach Bums or a guided charter will get you there in 20–30 minutes across Pine Island Sound. Go at low tide for best results and arrive early before other boaters reach the shells first.

Boat-Access-Only Islands Near Captiva

One of Captiva's biggest draws is the cluster of pristine, undeveloped islands reachable only by boat. A half-day rental from Beach Bums opens up an extraordinary range of destinations — no crowds, no development, just wild Florida coast.

🏖️ Cayo Costa State Park

9 miles of undeveloped Gulf beach, world-class shelling, camping. 20–30 min by boat from Captiva.

🌴 North Captiva Island

Private island community, no bridge. Secluded beaches and clear Gulf water. Great snorkeling at the pass.

🦅 Useppa Island

Historic private island with a storied past as a tarpon-fishing retreat. Beautiful gardens and old-Florida charm.

🦜 Cabbage Key

Famous for the Dollar Bill Bar — a restaurant plastered floor-to-ceiling with signed dollar bills. Accessible by boat tour or rental.

🐬 Punta Blanca Island

Uninhabited island in Pine Island Sound with excellent dolphin and manatee sightings in the surrounding shallows.

🎣 Matlacha Pass

Winding tidal pass connecting Pine Island Sound to Charlotte Harbor — one of the best inshore fishing corridors in SW Florida.

Captiva Water Sports Guide by Activity

Boat Rentals — Explore at Your Own Pace

A boat rental from Beach Bums is the most flexible way to experience Captiva's waters. The island's position at the northern end of Pine Island Sound gives renters immediate access to a vast, sheltered estuary perfect for first-time boaters, as well as Gulf passes and outer island runs for more experienced mariners. Standard half-day and full-day rentals are available; pontoon boats are well-suited for groups and families making the crossing to Cayo Costa.

Fishing — Pine Island Sound & Charlotte Harbor

Captiva's fishing is exceptional across a wide range of species and styles. Inshore anglers target the classic Pine Island Sound species — snook along the mangrove shorelines, redfish on the grass flats, sea trout near the shell bars, and the explosive tarpon run that passes through the Boca Grande Pass area in late spring and summer. Island Time Florida Charters specializes in guided inshore trips with captains who know the subtle tides and structure of these protected waters. Charlotte Harbor, accessible to the north, is one of the largest open-bay fishing grounds in all of Florida.

Jet Ski Tours — Pine Island Sound & Gulf Beaches

Jet ski rentals through Island Ski Rentals give a thrilling perspective on the barrier island chain. The calm, shallow waters of Pine Island Sound are ideal for intermediate riders, while the Gulf-side beaches offer long open stretches of clear water. Guided tours are the best option for those unfamiliar with the local tides and navigation — the channels between islands can be tricky without local knowledge, and manatee protection zones require extra awareness.

Wildlife Watching — Manatees, Dolphins & Roseate Spoonbills

Pine Island Sound is one of the richest wildlife corridors on Florida's Gulf Coast. Bottlenose dolphins are ubiquitous — it's common to see them bow-riding, feeding on the flats, or traveling in family groups through the sound. West Indian manatees frequent the sheltered coves and seagrass beds, especially October through April when they congregate near warm water refuges. Roseate spoonbills — stunning pink wading birds — are a regular sighting at sunset roosting sites throughout the estuary. Bald eagles and ospreys nest throughout the island chain year-round.

🐠 Manatee Awareness on Captiva Waters

West Indian manatees are a protected species that appear regularly in Pine Island Sound and the back-bay areas of Captiva. Florida law requires all boaters and jet ski operators to observe posted manatee protection zones, which may include slow-speed and no-wake requirements. These zones exist year-round or seasonally depending on location. Always watch for manatees before accelerating, especially in shallow grassy areas. Never approach, feed, or touch wild manatees.

Getting to Captiva Island

Captiva Island is accessed via the Sanibel Causeway from Fort Myers (toll road), then south to north across Sanibel via Periwinkle Way, and across Blind Pass Bridge to Captiva. From Fort Myers: about 45–60 minutes drive depending on causeway traffic. From Naples: about 90 minutes. From Sarasota: about 2 hours.

The Sanibel Causeway toll is $6–$8 per vehicle each way. Captiva has limited parking — arriving early (before 9 a.m.) is strongly recommended, especially in high season (January–April). Many visitors rent bikes or golf carts for getting around the island once parked.

Captiva Island Water Sports FAQ

What water sports are available on Captiva Island?

Captiva offers boat rentals, jet ski rentals and guided tours, inshore and nearshore fishing charters, dolphin and wildlife watching, shelling trips to Cayo Costa and North Captiva, kayaking in Pine Island Sound, parasailing, and sunset cruises. The island's location between the Gulf and the Sound gives access to a wide range of aquatic environments.

Can you rent a boat on Captiva Island?

Yes — Beach Bums Boat Rentals on Captiva Island offers a selection of boats for self-guided exploration. Rentals provide access to Pine Island Sound, the Gulf beaches, Cayo Costa State Park, North Captiva, and other nearby destinations. No boating license is required in Florida for most rental vessels under 26 feet for those born before 1988; a brief safety check may be required.

How do you get to Cayo Costa from Captiva?

Cayo Costa is accessible only by boat — there is no bridge or road access. A 20–30 minute ride across Pine Island Sound from Captiva brings you to the park's docking facilities. Boat rentals from Beach Bums give you the independence to make the crossing at your own schedule, or you can join a guided tour that includes the shelling run as part of the itinerary.

Is Captiva good for fishing?

Captiva Island is one of the finest fishing gateways in Southwest Florida. Pine Island Sound is a top inshore fishery for snook, redfish, sea trout, and tarpon. Charlotte Harbor to the north offers even more variety. The legendary Boca Grande Pass tarpon run — among the world's best — is within reach for a full-day charter. Island Time Florida Charters provides local expertise for all skill levels.

What is the difference between Captiva and Sanibel?

Sanibel is larger and more developed, with shops, restaurants, and the famous J.N. "Ding" Darling National Wildlife Refuge. Captiva is smaller and quieter, with a more exclusive resort feel. Both islands are renowned for shelling and wildlife. For water sports, Captiva is the better gateway — it sits closer to Pine Island Sound's best fishing and the boat-access-only islands like Cayo Costa.

Can you see manatees on Captiva Island?

Yes — manatees are regularly seen in the protected bays and seagrass beds of Pine Island Sound, especially October through April. The calm, warm back-bay waters near Captiva and the surrounding islands are ideal manatee habitat. Boat and jet ski operators in the area maintain awareness of manatee zones and can often point out where recent sightings have occurred.

What is the tarpon fishing like near Captiva?

The tarpon fishing near Captiva is legendary. Boca Grande Pass — just north of Captiva accessible by charter — is considered one of the premier tarpon fishing spots in the entire world. The spring–summer tarpon migration (April–July) brings massive schools of silver kings ranging from 80–200+ lbs through the pass. Inshore tarpon also frequent the Pine Island Sound grass flats throughout the warmer months. Island Time Florida Charters can arrange tarpon-focused trips.
