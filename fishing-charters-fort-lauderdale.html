Fort Lauderdale is one of Florida's premier fishing ports. The Gulf Stream runs within 2 miles of shore here — closer than almost anywhere else on the East Coast — putting world-class pelagic fishing within reach of even a short half-day trip. Between the offshore blue water, the nearshore reef system, and the Intracoastal canals with their snook and tarpon, Fort Lauderdale gives anglers more variety in a single day than most fishing destinations offer in a week.

🐟 What to Target Near Fort Lauderdale

Offshore — Gulf Stream (2–15 miles out)

Mahi-mahi are the top target April–August. Sailfish dominate November–March. Wahoo and kingfish run October–February. Blackfin tuna are available year-round just past the reef in 300–600 feet of water.

Nearshore Reefs & Wrecks

Fort Lauderdale's reef runs 1–4 miles offshore and is loaded with yellowtail snapper, mangrove snapper, grouper, and cobia. Many artificial reefs and wrecks have been added over the years, creating excellent structure for bottom fishing. Great for beginners and families on half-day trips.

Intracoastal & Canals (Inshore)

Fort Lauderdale's canal system holds snook, tarpon, jack crevalle, and ladyfish year-round. Light tackle and fly fishing for these species in an urban setting — cruising past million-dollar waterfront homes — is a uniquely Fort Lauderdale experience.

❓ Frequently Asked Questions

Where do charters depart from in Fort Lauderdale?

The main departure points are Bahia Mar Marina (Fort Lauderdale Beach Blvd), the Hillsboro Inlet in Pompano Beach, and Sands Harbor Marina. Most operators offer free parking at the marina.

Do I need a fishing license in Fort Lauderdale?

No. The captain's license covers all passengers when aboard a licensed charter. Bring your own snacks and drinks — or confirm if the charter provides them.
