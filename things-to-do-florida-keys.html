Things to Do in the Florida Keys 2026 — Complete Activity Guide

Updated April 2026 Key Largo · Islamorada · Marathon · Key West From $25

The Florida Keys stretch 120 miles from the southern tip of the Florida mainland to Key West, connected by the legendary Overseas Highway — one of the most scenic drives in America. Along the way you'll find tropical islands, the only coral reef in the continental US, world-class fishing, laid-back island towns, and some of the best sunsets on the planet. Here's a complete guide to the best activities in the Keys, organized by category.

🌊 Water Activities — The Best of the Keys

Reef Snorkeling & Diving

The Florida Reef is the most spectacular thing the Keys has to offer. Snorkel or dive at Molasses Reef (Key Largo), Cheeca Rocks (Islamorada), Looe Key (Big Pine Key), or the reefs off Key West. Visibility can reach 60–80 feet on calm days, with coral formations, tropical fish, sea turtles, and nurse sharks. Check out our full snorkeling guide for the Florida Keys.

Fishing

The Florida Keys are the "Sport Fishing Capital of the World." Every key along the chain offers something different — tarpon in Key West, bonefish and permit in Islamorada's backcountry, snapper on the Key Largo reefs. See our full Key West fishing charters guide or the complete Florida Keys fishing guide.

Kayaking & Paddleboarding

The calm Florida Bay side of the Keys is perfect for kayaking. Paddle through mangrove tunnels, spot herons and manatees, and access hidden sandbars that powerboats can't reach. John Pennekamp State Park in Key Largo and Curry Hammock State Park in Marathon are top launch points.

Sunset Sailing & Boat Tours

Key West's sunset at Mallory Square is famous — but watching it from a catamaran out on the water is a completely different (and better) experience. Multiple sunset cruises depart nightly from the Key West Historic Seaport, with options ranging from champagne catamaran tours to bare-bones sailing adventures.

🏝️ Must-See Places Along the Keys

John Pennekamp Coral Reef State Park (Key Largo, MM 102)

America's first underwater park. Glass-bottom boat tours, snorkel trips, and kayak rentals are all available directly from the park. Even non-swimmers can see the reef from the glass-bottom boat.

Theater of the Sea (Islamorada, MM 84)

One of the world's oldest marine mammal facilities. Swim with dolphins, sea lions, and stingrays in natural lagoons. A classic Keys experience, especially for families.

Seven Mile Bridge (Marathon, MM 47)

One of the most iconic drives in the US — seven miles over open ocean connecting Marathon to the lower Keys. The old bridge beside it is now a pedestrian walkway and fishing pier. Stop halfway for the view.

Bahia Honda State Park (Big Pine Key, MM 37)

Consistently rated one of the best beaches in Florida. Clear, calm water, excellent snorkeling directly from shore, kayak rentals, and camping. The remains of the old railroad bridge make for a dramatic backdrop.

Key West Historic District

The southernmost city in the continental US. Wander Duval Street, visit the Ernest Hemingway Home (he wrote A Farewell to Arms here), watch the sunset at Mallory Square, and experience the most unique small-city culture in America.

❓ Frequently Asked Questions

How many days do you need in the Florida Keys?

3–5 days is the sweet spot. That's enough time to experience Key Largo, Islamorada or Marathon, and at least one full day in Key West. A week lets you truly relax and explore each area at a slower pace.

Is it worth driving the Overseas Highway?

Absolutely — it's one of the great American road trips. Plan your stops along the way rather than rushing straight to Key West. Each community along the 120-mile chain has its own distinct character.

Is the Florida Keys expensive?

Key West especially can be pricey for accommodation. Budget travelers do better in the Upper or Middle Keys where lodging is more affordable. Water activities range from very affordable (kayak rental, shore snorkeling) to premium (private charters).
